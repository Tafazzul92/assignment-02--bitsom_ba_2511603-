## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, data integrity, consistency, and reliability are non negotiable. Patient records—such as medical history, prescriptions, allergies, diagnoses, and billing information—must be accurate at all times. In this context, a relational database like MySQL is the stronger choice because it provides ACID guarantees: Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, and Durability. These properties ensure that updates to patient data occur safely and predictably, even under concurrent access. For example, when updating a patient’s medication list, the system must never allow partial writes or conflicting updates. MySQL’s transactional model is designed precisely for such scenarios.
MongoDB, while flexible, follows the BASE philosophy (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventually consistent). Eventual consistency is unsuitable for healthcare, where stale or partially updated data could lead to harmful decisions. Under the CAP theorem, MongoDB prioritizes availability and partition tolerance, whereas a healthcare system must prioritize consistency above all else. For core patient management, MySQL aligns better with regulatory expectations and clinical safety requirements.
However, if the startup later adds a fraud detection module, the recommendation becomes hybrid. Fraud detection often involves large volumes of semi structured data, rapid ingestion, and flexible schema evolution—areas where MongoDB excels. Machine learning driven fraud analysis may require storing logs, device fingerprints, or behavioral patterns that do not fit neatly into relational tables.
